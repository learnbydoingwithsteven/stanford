# Stanford CS PhD Program Guide

**Complete Guide to Doctoral Studies in Computer Science**

---

## 🎓 Program Overview

The Stanford CS PhD program trains students to become world-class researchers who push the boundaries of computing. The program emphasizes original research, deep technical expertise, and scholarly contribution to the field.

### Program Characteristics
- **Duration**: 4-6 years (average: 5.5 years)
- **Cohort Size**: ~30-35 students/year
- **Faculty**: 50+ tenure-track professors
- **Research Areas**: 10+ major areas
- **Funding**: Full funding guaranteed for all admitted students

## 📋 Degree Requirements

### 1. **Coursework (135 units minimum)**

#### Foundation Requirements (same as MS)
- Logic, Automata & Complexity (CS103)
- Probability (CS109 or STATS 116)
- Algorithmic Analysis (CS161)
- Computer Organization & Systems (CS107)
- Principles of Computer Systems (CS110)

#### Breadth Requirements
- **3 courses** from different breadth areas (A, B, C, D)
- Same areas as MS program
- Typically completed in first year

#### Depth Requirements
- **21 units** in primary research area
- **15 units** at 200-level or higher
- B average or better

#### Research Units
- **CS390**: Research (variable units)
- Typically 9-15 units per quarter after coursework

### 2. **Teaching Requirement**
- **3 quarters** of teaching assistantship
- Usually completed in years 2-3
- 8-10 hours/week commitment
- Gain teaching experience and mentorship skills

### 3. **Qualifying Examination**
- **Timing**: End of 2nd year (or early 3rd year)
- **Format**: Oral exam (2-3 hours)
- **Committee**: 4-5 faculty members
- **Content**:
  - Research proposal presentation (30-45 min)
  - Questions on research area
  - Questions on breadth areas
  - Assessment of research potential
- **Outcome**: Pass, conditional pass, or fail
- **Preparation**: 3-6 months of intensive preparation

### 4. **Dissertation & Defense**
- **Dissertation**: Original research contribution
- **Length**: Typically 100-200 pages
- **Reading Committee**: 3 faculty members (advisor + 2 others)
- **Defense**: Oral defense (2-3 hours)
  - Public presentation (45-60 min)
  - Closed-door examination
- **Outcome**: Pass with minor revisions (typical), major revisions, or fail

### 5. **Residency Requirement**
- **Minimum**: 3 years of full-time study
- **On-campus**: At least 2 consecutive years
- **Exceptions**: Rare, requires petition

## 📊 Typical PhD Timeline

### Year 1: Coursework & Exploration
**Autumn Quarter**:
- 3 breadth/depth courses (9 units)
- Attend research group meetings
- Explore research areas
- Meet with potential advisors

**Winter Quarter**:
- 3 breadth/depth courses (9 units)
- Begin rotation projects (optional)
- Narrow down research interests
- Attend seminars and talks

**Spring Quarter**:
- 2-3 courses (6-9 units)
- Start research project
- Identify potential advisor
- Begin reading research papers

**Summer**:
- Research internship (optional but common)
- Or continue research at Stanford
- Prepare for 2nd year research

### Year 2: Research & Teaching
**Autumn Quarter**:
- 1-2 courses (3-6 units)
- Research (6-9 units)
- TA assignment (1st quarter)
- Work on research project

**Winter Quarter**:
- 1-2 courses (3-6 units)
- Research (6-9 units)
- TA assignment (2nd quarter)
- Prepare for qualifying exam

**Spring Quarter**:
- Research (9-12 units)
- TA assignment (3rd quarter)
- Qualifying exam preparation
- Write research proposal

**Summer**:
- Qualifying exam (typically August)
- Research internship (optional)
- Or continue research at Stanford

### Year 3: Deep Research
**Autumn Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Advance candidacy (after passing quals)
- Focus on dissertation research
- Submit first conference paper

**Winter Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Continue dissertation work
- Attend conferences
- Collaborate with lab members

**Spring Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Publish research papers
- Refine dissertation direction
- Consider internship

**Summer**:
- Research internship (optional)
- Or intensive research at Stanford
- Prepare papers for submission

### Year 4: Publication & Progress
**Autumn Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Submit papers to top conferences
- Expand dissertation scope
- Mentor junior students

**Winter Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Present at conferences
- Collaborate on projects
- Build academic profile

**Spring Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Continue publishing
- Assess dissertation progress
- Plan remaining work

**Summer**:
- Final research internship (optional)
- Or focus on dissertation
- Prepare for job market (if graduating next year)

### Year 5: Dissertation & Job Market
**Autumn Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Apply for academic/industry positions
- Give job talks
- Write dissertation chapters

**Winter Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Interview for positions
- Continue writing dissertation
- Finalize research contributions

**Spring Quarter**:
- Research (12-15 units)
- Complete dissertation writing
- Schedule defense
- Prepare defense presentation

**Summer**:
- Dissertation defense
- Final revisions
- Graduate!
- Start postdoc or industry position

### Year 6+ (if needed)
- Complete remaining research
- Finish dissertation writing
- Defend and graduate

## 💰 Funding & Stipend

### Guaranteed Funding Package
All admitted PhD students receive:

1. **Tuition Coverage**: Full tuition (~$60,000/year)
2. **Stipend**: ~$45,000-$50,000/year (2024-2025)
3. **Health Insurance**: Cardinal Care health plan
4. **Fees**: Most fees covered

### Funding Sources
- **Years 1-2**: Departmental fellowships
- **Years 3+**: Research assistantships (RA) from advisor's grants
- **Teaching**: TA positions (3 quarters required)
- **External Fellowships**: NSF GRFP, NDSEG, Hertz, etc.

### External Fellowships (Highly Recommended)
1. **NSF Graduate Research Fellowship (NSF GRFP)**:
   - $37,000/year stipend + tuition
   - 3 years of support
   - Apply in 1st or 2nd year
   - ~15% acceptance rate

2. **NDSEG Fellowship**:
   - Full tuition + stipend
   - 3 years of support
   - For US citizens only
   - Focus on defense-related research

3. **Hertz Fellowship**:
   - $38,000/year stipend + tuition
   - Up to 5 years
   - Highly competitive (~1% acceptance)
   - Focus on applied sciences

4. **Google PhD Fellowship**:
   - $50,000/year + travel
   - 2 years of support
   - For outstanding students in CS

5. **Facebook/Meta Fellowship**:
   - $42,000/year + travel
   - 2 years of support
   - For AI/ML students

### Additional Income
- **Summer Internships**: $15,000-$25,000 (3 months)
- **Conference Travel Grants**: $1,000-$3,000/conference
- **TA Positions**: Additional income beyond required quarters
- **Consulting**: With advisor approval

## 🎯 Admission Process

### Requirements
1. **Bachelor's or Master's Degree**: In CS or related field
2. **Strong Academic Record**: GPA 3.7+ (typical)
3. **Research Experience**: Publications, projects, or thesis
4. **Letters of Recommendation**: 3 required (from research advisors)
5. **Statement of Purpose**: Research interests and goals
6. **Transcripts**: Official transcripts
7. **GRE**: Not required (as of 2024)

### Application Timeline
- **Deadline**: December 1
- **Interviews**: January-February (by invitation)
- **Decisions**: March
- **Visit Days**: March-April
- **Acceptance Deadline**: April 15

### Admission Statistics
- **Applications**: ~2,500/year
- **Admits**: ~80-100/year
- **Enrollment**: ~30-35/year
- **Acceptance Rate**: ~3-4%
- **Yield**: ~35-40%

### What Makes a Strong Application?
1. **Research Experience**: 
   - Publications at top conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, CVPR, etc.)
   - Strong research projects with clear contributions
   - Research internships at top labs

2. **Strong Letters**:
   - From well-known researchers
   - Detailed and specific
   - Compare to other top students

3. **Clear Research Vision**:
   - Specific research interests
   - Alignment with faculty
   - Understanding of current research

4. **Academic Excellence**:
   - High GPA (3.8+)
   - Strong performance in core CS courses
   - Math/theory background

5. **Fit with Department**:
   - Multiple potential advisors
   - Research area alignment
   - Contribution to community

## 💡 Tips for Success

### Choosing an Advisor
- **Research Fit**: Align with your interests
- **Advising Style**: Match your working style
- **Funding**: Ensure stable funding
- **Placement**: Check advisor's student placements
- **Culture**: Lab culture and dynamics
- **Communication**: Regular meetings and feedback

### Research Strategy
- **Start Early**: Begin research in year 1
- **Publish Often**: Aim for 1-2 papers/year
- **Top Venues**: Target top conferences
- **Collaborate**: Work with others in lab
- **Iterate**: Don't get stuck on one idea
- **Read Widely**: Stay current with literature

### Coursework Strategy
- **Front-Load**: Take most courses in years 1-2
- **Breadth First**: Cover breadth requirements early
- **Depth Later**: Take depth courses as needed
- **Audit**: Audit courses after requirements met

### Networking
- **Conferences**: Attend and present at conferences
- **Workshops**: Participate in workshops
- **Internships**: Do 1-2 research internships
- **Collaborations**: Collaborate with other labs
- **Social**: Attend department events

### Time Management
- **Set Goals**: Weekly and quarterly goals
- **Track Progress**: Monitor research progress
- **Balance**: Maintain work-life balance
- **Deadlines**: Plan around conference deadlines
- **Breaks**: Take vacations and breaks

### Job Market Preparation
- **Start Early**: Begin preparing in year 4
- **Build Profile**: Publications, talks, collaborations
- **Job Talk**: Prepare compelling job talk
- **Research Statement**: Clear research vision
- **Network**: Connect with potential employers

## 🔄 Common Challenges & Solutions

### Challenge 1: Finding an Advisor
**Solution**: 
- Rotate with 2-3 advisors in year 1
- Attend multiple research group meetings
- Talk to current students about advisors
- Consider co-advising arrangements

### Challenge 2: Research Direction
**Solution**:
- Explore multiple directions early
- Don't commit too early
- Iterate on ideas quickly
- Seek feedback from advisor and peers

### Challenge 3: Publication Pressure
**Solution**:
- Focus on quality over quantity
- Target top venues
- Collaborate to increase productivity
- Don't compare yourself to others

### Challenge 4: Qualifying Exam
**Solution**:
- Start preparing 6 months early
- Form study groups
- Do practice presentations
- Get feedback from advisor

### Challenge 5: Work-Life Balance
**Solution**:
- Set boundaries
- Take regular breaks
- Maintain hobbies and social life
- Seek support when needed

### Challenge 6: Imposter Syndrome
**Solution**:
- Remember you were admitted for a reason
- Talk to peers (everyone feels this way)
- Celebrate small wins
- Seek mentorship and support
